200 to 32—why are lesbian bars closing?

Exploring factors that contribute to the sudden decline of lesbian bars in NYC a well as across the country

By Mariam Lobjanidze

As you stroll down Hudson street in New York City—the quaint, tree-lined west village area with a stronghold of LGBTQ+ history—you’ll see rainbow flags around at least every other block. Nestled on the intersection of Hudson street and Morton street is Henrietta Hudson, the oldest and one of 32 lesbian bars in the country that not only survived many challenges including COVID-19 but also claims to be on the most visited lGBTQ+ spaces in the city.

The history of lesbian spaces in the country is riddled with many ups and downs. In the 1980s, there were about 200 lesbian bars, whereas, as of 2024, the number is down to about 32. There are many speculations about reasons behind the trend including the inherent disadvantage that marginzalized communities have when owning a business. In particular, this can manifest itself in queer women being denied loans, according to Erica Rose, a queer filmmaker who launched the Lesbian Bar Project.

Henrietta Hudson is one of 3 lesbian bars in New York alongside Ginger’s and Cubbyhole.

“A lot of us grew up in environments where we didn’t even have anything close to what Hens or Cubby is or even some other queer spaces so the fact that there is only a few of them left in the country makes me cherish those spaces even more,” Nina Dvali, a Henrietta Hudson patreon.

“I think what some people don’t understand and I mean… if you haven’t been through certain struggles, I can see why that is, but the point is those bars are not just bars where they serve drinks. It takes so much dedication on the owners’ part to run those spaces because of anti-lgbtq legislation as well as disproportionate ways in which COVID-19 affected queer business owners,” Anne Ortiz, a Cubbyhole patreon.

“I think lesbian bars have seen a lot in terms of queer first dates, coming out stories, and I really do appreciate how staff and patrons try to take care of these space as much as they can,” Ortiz said.
